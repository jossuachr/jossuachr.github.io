DataFest 2025 – Best in Show

An end-to-end data analysis and modeling pipeline built for industry partners, earning the RStudio Wizards team Best in Show at DataFest Mizzou 2025.

Business Problem

Industry sponsors challenged us to analyze a complex multi-source dataset and deliver actionable insights on rental pricing dynamics across major U.S. markets. Specifically, we aimed to:

  • Identify key drivers of rental price variation.
  • Predict overall rent and flag under‑ or over‑valued leases.
  • Build an interactive dashboard for stakeholders to explore scenarios.

This project required blending statistical rigor with practical deployment to support real‑time decision making.

Data Description

We merged four primary datasets:

  • Leases: transaction records with rent amounts, lease durations, and identifiers.
  • Unemployment: state‑level quarterly unemployment rates.
  • Price & Availability: market occupancy proportions and availability metrics.
  • Major Market: geospatial definitions of markets with demographic attributes.

Key engineered variables included overall_rent (sum of lease payments), price_category (under vs over predicted rent), and market‑level summary statistics.

Data Wrangling 
# Merge datasets
leases_full <- Leases %>%
  merge(Unemployment, by=c("year","quarter","state"), all.x=TRUE) %>%
  merge(PriceAvailability, by=c("year","quarter","market"), all.x=TRUE) %>%
  merge(MajorMarket, by=c("year","quarter","market"), all.x=TRUE)

# Compute overall rent and price category
leases_clean <- leases_full %>%
  group_by(lease_id) %>%
  summarize(overall_rent = sum(rent_amount), .groups='drop') %>%
  left_join(leases_full, by='lease_id') %>%
  mutate(
    price_category = ifelse(predicted_rent > overall_rent, 'Under', 'Over')
  )
Model Training & Tuning 
# Train-test split
data_split <- initial_split(leases_clean, strata=price_category)
train <- training(data_split); test <- testing(data_split)

# Recipe & model spec
rf_recipe <- recipe(overall_rent ~ unemployment_rate + avg_occupancy_proportion + leasedSF + availability_proportion,
                    data=train) %>%
  step_impute_median(all_numeric_predictors()) %>%
  step_normalize(all_numeric_predictors())

rf_spec <- rand_forest(mode='regression', trees=1000, mtry=tune(), min_n=tune()) %>%
  set_engine('ranger')

wf <- workflow() %>% add_recipe(rf_recipe) %>% add_model(rf_spec)

# Cross-validation & tuning
grid <- grid_random(mtry(range=c(2,5)), min_n(range=c(5,20)), size=20)
cv <- vfold_cv(train, v=5)
tune_res <- tune_grid(wf, resamples=cv, grid=grid, metrics=metric_set(rsq, rmse))

best <- select_best(tune_res, 'rmse')
final_wf <- finalize_workflow(wf, best)
final_fit <- last_fit(final_wf, data_split)

Modeling Results

On the held-out test set, our optimized Random Forest delivered:

  • R²: 0.85
  • RMSE: $45

These metrics indicate strong predictive power and low residual error for rental pricing.

Key Insights & Recommendations

Our analysis surfaced several strategic insights:

  • Markets with higher median income correlate with significantly higher rents.
  • Rising unemployment rates have a dampening but nonlinear effect on pricing.
  • Occupancy rates remain a strong leading indicator of price trends.

We recommend integrating our model into a Plumber API for real‑time rent valuation and enhancing the Shiny dashboard for scenario planning, enabling stakeholders to simulate the impact of economic shifts on lease pricing.
